## My Life Before Islam

Muhammad Ibrahim, born Reese Byford, grew up in London in a household where there was a strong, culturally ingrained dislike for Islam, often fueled by negative media portrayals. He describes his younger self as "lost" and "disrespectful," particularly toward his parents, frequently engaging in arguments and disobedience. Around the age of 13, he began a spiritual quest, feeling that life must have a purpose and a Creator. He briefly explored Judaism, ancient Egyptian religions, Buddhism, and spent a year deeply researching Hinduism, but none provided the logical "grounding" he was looking for.

## The Moment of Guidance

His journey toward Islam took six years of research. While attending college, where roughly 90% of the students were Muslim, he began to study the Quran. He was initially struck by the simplicity and depth of Surah Al-Fatiha and Surah Al-Baqarah, eventually becoming "addicted" to reading 10 to 15 pages a day. The scientific accuracy regarding the creation of the universe and the human embryo convinced him that the Quran could not have been written by a man in the desert 1,400 years ago. He embraced Islam at age 17. His transition was challenging; his father discovered his "topi" (prayer cap), leading to several weeks of silence between them, and his first year as a Muslim was marked by physical hardship, sometimes going days without food because he didn't know what was permissible to eat.

## Reflections

Muhammad Ibrahim finds profound justice in Islamic rulings, which he believes look at society from every angle. He credits a close friend, Muhammad (a born Muslim), for helping him through his first Ramadan and for opening his home for Iftar. Interestingly, Ibrahim's constant questions pushed his friend to study his own religion more deeply. Ibrahim has since left his university degree to pursue Islamic studies full-time, enrolling in Arabic intensive courses. His dream is to become a scholar (Alim) and dedicate his life to pleasing his Creator. He advises those born into Islam to value their faith like a "diamond," as he feels a sense of urgency to catch up on the years of knowledge he missed.
