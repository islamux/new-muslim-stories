## My Life Before Islam

Margaret was born in 1957 in Manchester, England, and raised in the Church of England. As she grew older, she began to struggle with certain core Christian doctrines, particularly the concept of the Trinity, which she felt lacked logical consistency. When she sought answers from religious figures, she was often brushed off, leading her to lose her faith. In 1975, she met her future husband and later traveled to Iraq. It was there, amidst the daily sounds of the Adhan (call to prayer) and the warmth of her Iraqi family, that she felt a deep spiritual connection. She took her Shahada in Baghdad at the time of her marriage, though she notes that her true understanding of Islam developed through years of personal study and life experience.

## The Moment of Guidance

Margaret's commitment to her faith was tested by immense personal hardship. After her husband left her, she made a firm decision to raise her children as Muslims and to start wearing the Hijab, despite the lack of Islamic resources and social support in Manchester at the time. She faced significant public abuse, including being spat at and harassed, requiring police protection for a year. However, her faith was solidified by a specific verse in the Quran: "On no soul does Allah place a burden greater than it can bear" (Surah Al-Baqarah, 2:286). This verse became her anchor through some of the darkest moments of her life.

## Reflections

Margaret's story is one of incredible resilience. She has survived cancer and a stroke, and even when she lost her ability to speak during the stroke, she kept repeating "Alhamdulillah" (Praise be to God) in her mind. She reflects on how Islam changed her from being focused on material things to becoming a more serious and peaceful person. Her greatest wish is to establish a lending library in Manchester where new Muslims can study and discuss their faith together. She encourages others to ask sincere questions about Islam rather than relying on media portrayals, and she remains steadfast in her belief that every hardship is a means of spiritual growth.
